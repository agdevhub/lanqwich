Actually, yes! It is with the help of demonstrative pronouns that we point to specific things: this, that, these, and those, as in “This is an apple,” “Those are boys,” or “Take these to the clerk.”

What is this?

مَا هَذَا؟

This is a book.

هَذَا كِتَابٌ

Is this a house?

أَهَذَا بَيْتٌ؟

The particle أ placed at the beginning of a statement turns it into a question.

Yes, this is a house

نَعَمْ، هَذَا بَيْتٌ

No, this is a mosque.

لا، هَذَا مَسْجِدٌ

Who is this?

مَنْ هَذَا؟

I guess, looking at the examples, you've already figured out that Arabic has no word corresponding to the English "is". As such, Arabic has no copula.

There is no word in Arabic corresponding to "a" in English as in "this is a book." The n-sound at the end of the Arabic noun (kitaabu-n, baitu-n, masjidu-n) is the Arabic indefinite article corresponding to the English "a"/"an". This sound is called tanwin.

Do you know that we are using Naskh style for Arabic texts on our website? Naskh is a smaller, round script of Islamic calligraphy. Naskh is one of the first scripts of Islamic calligraphy to develop, commonly used in writing administrative documents and for transcribing books, including the Qur’an, because of its easy legibility.
